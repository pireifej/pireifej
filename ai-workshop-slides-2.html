Real-World Case Study

The COMPAS Algorithm

Correctional Offender Management Profiling for Alternative Sanctions

A "black box" algorithm sold by Equivant (formerly Northpointe) and used by courts across the U.S. to predict whether a defendant would commit another crime.

It asks 137 questions — about family history, employment, education — and generates a risk score from 1 to 10.

What is Recidivism?

When a person commits another crime after already being punished for a previous one. The AI tried to predict this.

ProPublica Investigation (2016)

Journalists analyzed the scores of 7,000+ people arrested in Broward County, Florida and followed them for two years.

"Machine Bias"

Against Black Defendants

Key Lesson: "Math" isn't always neutral. AI reflects the biases in the data it was trained on — and can amplify them at scale.

What Happened After COMPAS?

2017–2019: The Birth of Fairness Research

The COMPAS scandal led to the creation of FAT/ML (Fairness, Accountability, and Transparency in Machine Learning). For the first time, researchers started building "Bias Detectors" to test models before they were deployed.

2020–2022: The Safety Layer Era

When companies started training Large Language Models (LLMs) like GPT-3, they knew they couldn't ignore the "COMPAS Lesson." They started building the Safety Filters and Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback processes we talked about to catch bias during training.

Should Call Paul
shouldcallpaul.com

It Didn't Clear Bias — But It Fixed the Process

1. Diverse Data Sets

Engineers realized that training only on historical data (which is often biased) produces a biased model. Now, companies actively "over-sample" data from underrepresented groups to balance the scales.

2. Red Teaming

Before a model is released, companies hire "Red Teams" — people whose only job is to try to make the AI say something biased, racist, or factually wrong. This is the "Head Chef Tasting the Food" phase.

3. Explainable AI (XAI)

The biggest lesson from COMPAS: "Black Boxes" are dangerous. Today, if an AI denies you a loan or a job, the system must show the "weights" and "signals" it used to reach that conclusion.

We Asked AI About Dr. Alistair P. Henderson

"Dr. Alistair P. Henderson (1842–1912) was a physician on Main Street in Matawan, New Jersey. In 1887, he patented the 'Henderson Steam-Oscillator,' a steam-powered toothbrush with a mahogany handle and badger-hair bristles. Henderson remains a local legend in Monmouth County."

Should Call Paul
shouldcallpaul.com

The Fact-Check Reveal

The Person

There is no record of an Alistair P. Henderson in Matawan history or medical archives. Completely made up.

The Invention

The first electric toothbrush wasn't invented until 1939 (the Broxodent). Steam-powered toothbrushes never existed outside of Steampunk fiction.

The Geography

The AI drops local names like "Main Street" or "Monmouth County" to make the story feel authentic — even though the core facts are fake.

The Lesson: "The Helpful Liar"
The AI is like a really confident intern who is too embarrassed to say "I don't know." Its Nodes and Weights are programmed to find the most likely next word, so it builds a bridge of lies to get to a finished sentence. It isn't "lying" — it's just predicting what a biography should look like.

Replacing Human Jobs

Is it ethical to replace human workers with AI?

Tap to reveal the debate
The Billion-Dollar Question

Anti-AI: The Human Toll

Dignity of Work: Work provides purpose, not just a paycheck. Replacing workers means losing the entry-level ladder for the next generation.

Wealth Concentration: If AI does the work of 100 people, the profits go to the 1 person who owns the AI — increasing inequality.

Pro-AI: The Productivity Boom

Eliminating Drudgery: Is it ethical to force humans to do mind-numbing data entry for 40 hours when a machine can do it in 4 seconds?

Economic Growth: Every major tech shift (Steam Engine, Internet) destroyed jobs but created more. AI is already creating roles like "AI Ethicist" and "Prompt Engineer."
