Recommendation Systems

How Does YouTube Know What You Like?

YouTube's recommendation engine uses collaborative filtering — it finds people with similar tastes to you and recommends what they watched. It analyzes over 80 billion signals every day, including watch time, likes, shares, and even how fast you scroll past a video.

Key Insight: The AI doesn't "understand" the videos — it just finds patterns in what millions of people watch.

How AI Models Work

How a Model Is Built (4 Steps)

AI models like ChatGPT and Gemini don't "think" like a human. They predict the most likely next word based on patterns learned from billions of pages of text. Building a model takes four steps:

  • Pre-Training: The model reads trillions of words from the open internet — Common Crawl, Wikipedia, books, scientific papers. It learns patterns in language but has no concept of right or wrong.
  • Post-Training (RLHF): Thousands of human data labelers rank the model's answers: "This is helpful" vs "This is a hallucination." The model learns what humans actually want through Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback.
  • The Model (LLM): The model is now frozen in time with a knowledge cutoff. It doesn't learn from your chat in real-time — it predicts based on everything it has already learned.
  • Deployment: The model is released to the world via apps and APIs. Gemini, ChatGPT, Claude — they all went through these same four steps.

Think of it like a chef: Pre-training = reading every recipe book. Post-training = food critics taste-testing dishes. The Model = a trained chef with real feedback. Deployment = the restaurant opens.

AI Ethics & The COMPAS Case

When AI Gets It Wrong

COMPAS (Correctional Offender Management Profiling for Alternative Sanctions) was a "black box" algorithm used by U.S. courts to predict whether a defendant would commit another crime. It asked 137 questions and generated a risk score from 1 to 10.

In 2016, ProPublica investigated over 7,000 cases in Broward County, Florida, and found serious racial bias:

  • Black defendants were 2x more likely to be incorrectly labeled "high risk" when they never re-offended
  • White defendants were more likely to be incorrectly labeled "low risk" even when they did commit more crimes

"Math" isn't always neutral. AI reflects the biases in the data it was trained on — and can amplify them at scale.

Prompt Engineering

The Prompt Formula

Getting great results from AI is all about how you ask. Use this formula:

  • Role: Tell the AI who to be ("You are an expert travel planner...")
  • Task: Be specific about what you want ("Create a 3-day itinerary for...")
  • Format: Tell it how to structure the answer ("Use bullet points, include costs...")
  • Constraints: Set boundaries ("Keep it under 200 words, family-friendly...")

A vague prompt gives a vague answer. A specific prompt gives a useful answer.

Quiz: Which is a better prompt?

B "You are a veterinarian. Write 3 tips for first-time puppy owners in bullet points, each under 20 words."

Bonus Challenge: Write Your Own Prompt

Pick a topic you care about (a hobby, a school subject, your favorite sport) and write a prompt using the formula: Role + Task + Format + Constraints. Then try it out on ChatGPT, Gemini, or another AI and see how the response compares to a simple, vague prompt!
