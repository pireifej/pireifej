Focus: Vocal Variety & Body Language Duration: 60 Minutes

1. Physical & Vocal Warm-up
The "Big Stretch": Reach high, then hang low to loosen the "instrument" (the body).
The "Accordion" Breath: Deep rib-expansion breaths to power the voice.
The Vocal Siren: Sliding from low to high pitch to stretch the range.
Raspberries - lip trills
The "Volume Dial": Practice projecting from a level 1 (whisper) to a level 5 (back-of-the-room power).
2. Prepared Speeches
Timer: 1.5 Minutes per student.
Use the same timer you implemented for the previous sessions
3. Vocal Variety
The Volume Slider: * The Whisper (Level 1): Ask the kids: "When do you whisper?" (Secrets, scary moments, surprises). Teach them that whispering actually makes the audience lean in closer to listen.
The Megaphone (Level 5): Ask: "When do you yell?" (Excitement, anger, cheering). Teach them to use this when they reach the "Boss Battle" or the climax of their story.
The Speed Pedal (Pace):
The Cheetah (Fast): Use this for action! "I grabbed the controller, I hit the button, the screen flashed, and BOOM!" Speaking fast makes the audience's heart beat faster.
The Turtle (Slow): Use this for serious moments, or right before a big reveal. "I opened the door... and there it was." Pausing and speaking slowly builds suspense.
The Character Switch (Voices):
Tell them: "You are the narrator of your speech. But sometimes, other characters talk."
Demonstrate changing your posture and voice to play a different character for just one sentence. (e.g., “My mom put her hands on her hips and said, [strict adult voice] 'Clean your room!'”)

Group Exercise: "The Mystery Tone" (10 Minutes)
This exercise helps kids understand how pitch and inflection change the meaning of a sentence.
The Sentence: "I can't believe you did that."
The Game: Give a student a "Secret Tone Card" (or whisper it to them). They must say the sentence using that emotion, and the group has to guess what it is.
Tones to Use: * Surprised/Excited (Winning a prize)
Sarcastic (A friend did something silly)
Suspicious (A detective solving a crime)
Exhausted (Just finished a marathon)

4. Body Language

Base Camp (The Resting Position):
Explain: "When you aren't doing anything in a game, your character stands in their 'idle animation.' Your idle animation is Base Camp."
How to do it: Feet shoulder-width apart. Hands drop totally relaxed to the side. No pockets. No crossing arms. No wringing hands. "If you don't know what to do with your hands, send them to Base Camp."
The 3 Tool Gestures: Teach them that hands are tools to help the audience see the words.
The Number Tool: If you say "I have three reasons," you must hold up 3 fingers.
The Size Tool: If something is tiny, pinch your fingers. If it is massive, stretch your arms as wide as they go.
The Heart Tool: If you are talking about yourself, your feelings, or your opinion, bring one hand to your chest.
Stage Movement: The "Walk and Plant"
The Mistake: Show them "The Tiger in the Cage" (pacing back and forth nervously without stopping).
The Fix: "Walk on a period." Tell them to stand in the center for their first point. When they transition to their next point, take three steps to the left, plant their feet like tree roots, and stay there. Never speak while your feet are moving aimlessly.

Body Language Exercise: "The Silent Storyteller" (10 Minutes)
This exercise focuses on non-verbal cues and making "intentional" movements on stage.
The Game: A student must act out a specific action or complex idea for 30 seconds without making a single sound.
Prompt Ideas:
"You are trying to walk a very tiny, energetic dog."
"You just walked into a room that smells like old socks."
"You are a scientist who just discovered a new species of bug."
"You are trying to build a Lego set, but the instructions are in a different language."
Discussion: After each turn, ask the group: "What did they do with their hands or face that helped you understand the story?"

5. Closing: "The Mirror Challenge" (5 Minutes)
Have students pair up. One person makes a facial expression or gesture (Confident, Thinking, Nervous), and the other must mirror it perfectly.
The Takeaway: Remind them that their face and hands "speak" just as loudly as their voice.
