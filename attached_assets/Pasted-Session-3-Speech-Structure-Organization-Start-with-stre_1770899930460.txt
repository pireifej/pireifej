Goal: Learn to organize ideas logically so the audience can follow along.
Theme: "The Roadmap."
Duration: 60 Minutes

I. Opening: The Jumbled Speech (5 Minutes)
Goal: Demonstrate why order matters.
The Demo: Start speaking to the class in a completely nonsensical order: "In conclusion, that is why I love summer. My name is Paul. First, the weather is warm. Today I’m talking about seasons."

The Discussion: Ask the students: "What was wrong with that? Why was it hard to listen to?"
The Takeaway: Explain that a speech is like a journey. If the directions are out of order, the audience gets lost. Today, we are learning how to draw the map.

II. Part 1: The Introduction (15 Minutes)
Goal: Master the art of the "Hook."
The Concept: Never start with "Hi, my name is..." or "Today I'm going to talk about..." You must grab their attention in the first 10 seconds.
The 3 Main Hook Types:
The Question: "Have you ever wondered what it would be like to live underwater?"
The Amazing Fact: "There are more stars in the sky than grains of sand on every beach on Earth."
The Story: "I woke up at 6:00 AM, opened my door, and realized I was in the wrong house."
Exercise: "Boring to Bold"
Give the class a mundane object (e.g., A Pencil or Socks).
Ask students to come up with one of the three hooks for a speech about that object.
Example (Socks): "Did you know the average person loses 50 socks a year in the laundry?"

The Pencil
The Question: "How many mistakes do you think this one pencil can fix?"
The Fact: "Did you know a single pencil can write 45,000 words?"
The Story: "I was halfway through my test when suddenly—SNAP—my lead broke."
2. A Water Bottle
The Question: "What is the one thing you can’t live more than three days without?"
The Fact: "Did you know that 70% of your brain is actually made of water?"
The Story: "I was running in the sun, my throat was bone dry, and then I saw it: a cold bottle of water."
3. A Pair of Sneakers
The Question: "What’s the first thing you put on before you go outside to play?"
The Fact: "Did you know that before sneakers were invented, both shoes were shaped exactly the same?"
The Story: "I tied my laces tight, stood at the starting line, and waited for the whistle to blow."
4. An Eraser
The Question: "Who here has ever made a mistake?" (Wait for hands). "That’s why this is the most important tool in your bag."
The Fact: "Before erasers were invented, people used pieces of bread to wipe away pencil marks."
The Story: "I looked at my drawing and hated it. One swipe of this, and the mistake was gone."
5. A Backpack
The Question: "If your backpack could talk, what secrets would it tell about you?"
The Fact: "Did you know the average school bag weighs as much as a heavy bowling ball?"
The Story: "I zipped up my bag, pulled on the straps, and realized... I forgot my lunch.”

III. Part 2: The Body & Transitions (15 Minutes)
Goal: Organizing main points using the "Rule of Three."
The Rule of Three: Our brains remember things in groups of three. Every speech should have three main "stops" on the map.
The Bridges (Transitions): Explain that you need "signposts" to tell the audience you are moving to a new point.
Keywords: "First," "Moving on to," "In addition," "Finally."
Activity: "Connect the Dots"
Write two unrelated words on the board (e.g., "Apples" and "Space Travel").
Challenge a student to create a transition sentence that connects them.
Example: "Apples are a healthy snack on Earth, but they are even more important for astronauts traveling to Mars."

Dogs and Pizza
The Bridge: "Dogs are my favorite animals, but they should never eat my favorite food: Pizza."
2. Video Games and Homework
The Bridge: "I love playing Video Games, but I have to finish my Homework before I can turn on the console."
3. Sharks and Toothbrushes
The Bridge: "Sharks have hundreds of sharp teeth, which is why they’d need a giant Toothbrush to keep them clean."
4. Snow and Ice Cream
The Bridge: "Both Snow and Ice Cream are freezing cold, but only one of them tastes good with chocolate sprinkles."
5. Basketball and YouTube
The Bridge: "I spent all day practicing Basketball, and now I’m going to watch the highlights on YouTube.”

IV. Part 3: The Conclusion (10 Minutes)
Goal: End with a "Mic Drop" instead of a fade-out.
The Concept: Never end with "So... yeah" or "I'm done."
The Two-Step Conclusion:
The Summary: Remind them of your three main points.
The Call to Action: Tell the audience exactly what you want them to do or think now.
Example: "We talked about why soccer is fun, healthy, and social. So, grab a ball and head to the field this weekend!"

V. Individual Workshop: "Mapping Your Speech" (15 Minutes)
Goal: Students draft a basic outline for a 1-minute speech.
The Topic: "The Most Important Invention Ever."
The Outline Task:
Introduction: Write your Hook.
Body Point 1: First reason why it's important.
Body Point 2: Second reason.
Body Point 3: Third reason.
Conclusion: Your final "Call to Action" sentence.
Sharing: Invite 3-4 students to stand up and read their "Roadmap" (just the outline points) to the class.

VI. Closing Ritual (5 Minutes)
Review: Ask the class, "What are the three types of hooks?" and "What is the Rule of Three?"
Homework: Tell students to listen to the next person they hear giving a presentation (or a video) and identify their "Hook."
The Sign-Off: A unified chant to end the session.
"1, 2, 3... DRAW THE MAP!”
