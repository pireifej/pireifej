The Defense Shift: Explain that we are moving from Detection (trying to spot the lie) to Verification (using cryptographic keys and "Zero Trust" protocols).
The "Aha!" Moment: Search and Strategy
The biggest technical shift isn't the neural network itself; it's "Inference-Time Compute."
In the past, we thought the only way to make AI smarter was to make the model bigger (more nodes). OpenAI discovered that you can make AI smarter by letting it "think longer" (spend more compute power) while it’s answering.
The o1 model uses its "nodes" to:
Backtrack: If its internal tokens start leading to a logical dead end, it is trained to "realize" this and try a different path.
Self-Correct: It predicts tokens that evaluate its own previous tokens (e.g., "Wait, I missed an 'R' in the first syllable").
Search: It essentially performs a "search" through different logical possibilities before committing to the final output.

Activity 3: The "Human Fireball" (Live Hot-Seat)

How are these fake pictures generated?
The images on this specific website are generated using a technology called GAN (Generative Adversarial Network)—specifically a version developed by NVIDIA called StyleGAN2.
To explain this to your class (using the terminology you’ve already taught them), you can break it down into a "Digital Duel":
1. Two Neural Networks at War
Instead of just one AI trying to predict a face, a GAN uses two separate neural networks that compete against each other:
The Generator: This network's only job is to create a fake image from random noise (random tokens/pixels). At first, it’s terrible and just makes static.
The Discriminator: This network is the "security guard." It has been trained on millions of real human faces. Its job is to look at an image and say, "That’s fake."
2. The Feedback Loop
The Generator shows a fake face to the Discriminator.
If the Discriminator catches the fake, the Generator "learns" from its mistake (adjusting its weights and nodes).
The Generator tries again, making the image slightly more realistic.
This happens millions of times until the Generator becomes so good at predicting the "next pixel" that the Discriminator can no longer tell the difference.
3. The "Latent Space" (The Library of Faces)
The AI isn't "copy-pasting" eyes and noses. It has mapped out a mathematical "space" of what a human face looks like. By changing a few numbers in its code, it can transition a face from "young" to "old" or "male" to "female."
Why the artifacts exist: Artifacts happen because the GAN is essentially a master of math, but not a master of logic. It knows that "pixels usually look like this near an ear," but it doesn't understand that humans are symmetrical or that earrings come in pairs.

Tips for the "Artifact Hunt"
When you call someone up, tell them not to look at the face as a whole (which is designed to fool the brain’s pattern recognition). Instead, tell them to scan the edges and "non-human" details:
The "Third Person" Glitch: Look at the background. AI often generates "flesh blobs" or distorted partial faces near the edges of the frame that look like a person melting into the background.
Earrings and Glasses: AI struggles with symmetry. Check if one earring is a hoop and the other is a stud, or if the frames of the glasses "merge" into the skin or have different shapes on the left and right.
The "Void" in the Eyes: Look at the specular highlights (the tiny white dots of reflected light). In a real photo, the reflection in both eyes should match the room's light source. In older AI models, these dots are often mismatched or misshapen.
Hair Messiness: Look at individual strands of hair. Real hair is chaotic but follows physics. AI hair often turns into a "pixel soup" or looks like it is painted onto the forehead rather than growing out of it.
Background Surrealism: The background is usually a blurred mess of colors that don't represent a real place (e.g., a window that leads to a brick wall, or a tree branch that turns into a car tire).
Also occlusion" (when one object is in front of another), AI struggles with this like a microphone in the foreground or helmet straps.
