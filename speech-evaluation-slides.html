The Full Speaker’s Warm-Up

10 Minutes
Session 6 Warm-Up

Today we run through the full speaker’s vocal & physical warm-up routine from Sessions 1–5. You know the drill — let’s get your body and voice ready!

1. The “Big Stretch”

Reach for the ceiling on tip-toes, stretching as tall as you can. Then drop and hang like a ragdoll, arms dangling loose. Shake it out!

2. The “Accordion” Breath

Put your hands on your ribs. Inhale for 4 counts (feel your ribs expand outward), hold for 4 counts, then exhale for 8 counts on a long “hisssss.” This deep rib-expansion breath powers your voice!

3. The Vocal Siren

Start at your lowest comfortable pitch on an “ooo” sound and slide smoothly to your highest note and back down, like a fire truck. Wheeee-ooooo! This stretches the vocal cords and increases range.

4. Raspberries & Lip Trills

Brrrrrrr! Blow raspberries to loosen up your lips and get the vibrations going. Then try humming while you do it.

5. The “Volume Dial”

Pick a simple sentence: “Public speaking is a superpower.”

  • Say it at Volume 1 (whisper)
  • Say it at Volume 3 (conversational)
  • Say it at Volume 5 (speaking to the back of the room)

What Is a Speech Evaluation?

5 Minutes
Definition

An evaluation is a thoughtful review of someone’s speech that helps them grow — not a grade, not a judgment, but a gift.

Why Do We Evaluate?

  • We can’t see ourselves speak — evaluators are our mirror
  • Every professional speaker has a coach or evaluator
  • Feedback helps us improve faster than practicing alone
  • Learning to evaluate others makes you a better speaker too

Be Kind

Your words should build someone up, not tear them down. Focus on encouragement.

Be Specific

Don’t just say “good job.” Say what was good and why it worked.

Be Helpful

Give one concrete thing they can try next time to improve.

“A great evaluator doesn’t just tell you what went wrong — they help you see what’s possible.”

The Feedback Sandwich

10 Minutes
The Golden Rule of Feedback

Wrap your suggestion between two compliments. This keeps the speaker feeling encouraged while still helping them grow.

Top Bread: What You Did Well

“I loved how you started with that funny story — it grabbed everyone’s attention right away!”

The Filling: One Thing to Try Next Time

“Next time, try slowing down during your main point so we can really let it sink in.”

Bottom Bread: Encouragement

“Overall, your confidence was amazing — I can tell you practiced and I can’t wait to hear your next speech!”

Feedback vs. Criticism

Not Helpful

“That was boring.”

“You messed up a lot.”

“I didn’t like it.”

Helpful Feedback

“Your ending was strong! Try making more eye contact during the middle part. You have a great speaking voice!”

Pro Tip

Start your feedback with the speaker’s name. It makes it personal and shows you’re speaking directly to them.

Evaluation Practice

10 Minutes
Your Evaluation Checklist

When you listen to a speech, pay attention to these areas:

Volume — Could you hear them clearly?
Eye Contact — Did they look at the audience?
Body Language — Did they use gestures?
Structure — Did it have a beginning, middle, end?
Hook — Did the opening grab your attention?
Feeling — How did the speech make you feel?

Live Evaluation Round

Two volunteers give a short 1-minute speech. After each speech, the audience practices giving feedback using the Feedback Sandwich.

  • Raise your hand to share your evaluation
  • Start with the speaker’s name
  • Use the sandwich: compliment → suggestion → encouragement
  • Be specific — name the exact moment you noticed

Practice Prompt

“Think of something you did for the first time. How did it go? What did you learn?”

How to Receive Feedback

5 Minutes
The Other Side

Giving feedback is a skill — but so is receiving it with grace.

Listen Fully

Don’t interrupt. Let the evaluator finish their thought completely before responding.

Say “Thank You”

Even if you disagree. Someone took the time to help you — that’s a gift worth appreciating.

Don’t Defend

It’s natural to want to explain, but try to just absorb the feedback first. Process it later.

Write It Down

Keep a feedback notebook. Write down one thing to work on from each evaluation you receive.

The “Thank You” Challenge

Practice receiving feedback right now! Turn to a partner, give them one compliment about their speaking, and the receiver must simply say: “Thank you!”

No “oh, it wasn’t that good” or “really?” — just a genuine thank you.

“Feedback is not about being perfect. It’s about getting better — one speech at a time.”
