Securing the Future: Homework

Review the new threats from generative AI — phishing, deepfakes, jailbreaks — and the modern defenses that keep you safe. How well can you spot a fake?

The New Threat Landscape

Why AI Changes the Game

For decades, scams were easy to spot — bad grammar, weird phrasing, obvious typos. Generative AI flipped that. Today, an attacker with a free chatbot can write a perfectly polished phishing email in seconds, clone a voice from a 3-second clip, or generate a face that has never existed.

This session was about three big shifts:

  • Scale: One attacker can now run thousands of personalized scams at once.
  • Quality: Fakes look and sound real — even to careful eyes.
  • Speed: Frontier models can find and exploit security holes in minutes, not weeks.

Old rule: “If it looks fishy, it’s phishy.” New rule: “Verification over trust.” The fake is now polished. Your only edge is to slow down and check.

Phishing & Social Engineering

Spot the Red Flags — Fake Email

Even a polished AI phishing email leaves fingerprints. Learn the four red flags we covered in the “Email A” exercise:

Fake Domain

bankofamerica-alerts.com is NOT bankofamerica.com. Attackers add words and dashes to look real.

Urgency Pressure

“Verify within 24 hours or your account will be suspended.” Real banks don’t threaten in this tone.

Generic Greeting

“Dear Valued Customer” instead of your name. Real institutions know who you are.

Suspicious Link

The link goes to boa-secure-verify.com — not bankofamerica.com. Always hover before you click.

Spot the Green Flags — Real Email

Legitimate alerts (like Email B from Chase) have signals that fakes can’t fake easily:

Legit Domain

no-reply@chase.com — the actual root domain, not a look-alike.

Specific Device

“iPhone 16, Safari, Red Bank NJ” — details only your real bank would have.

Uses Your Name

“Hi Paul” instead of “Dear Customer.” A small thing, but a real signal.

Phone #, Not a Link

A real call-back number you can dial yourself — not a clickable button that hides where you’re going.

Deepfakes & GANs

How Fake Faces Get Made

Deepfake faces come from a GAN — a Generative Adversarial Network. Two neural networks compete:

  • The Generator: Tries to create a fake face from random noise. Bad at first.
  • The Discriminator: A “security guard” trained on real faces. Tries to spot the fakes.

They duel millions of times. Each round, the Generator gets better at fooling the Discriminator. Eventually, the Generator can produce faces that no human (and no other AI) can tell from real.

The Artifact Hunt: How to Spot a Fake

GANs are masters of math, not logic. They know what pixels “usually” look like — but they don’t understand symmetry, physics, or context. So scan the edges and non-human details:

  • Earrings & glasses: One hoop and one stud? Frames that merge into the skin?
  • The eyes: Both eyes should reflect the same light source. Fakes get this wrong.
  • Hair: Real hair is messy but follows physics. AI hair turns into “pixel soup.”
  • Background: Can you name a single object in the background? If it’s all blur and color, it’s suspicious.
  • The “Third Person” glitch: Look for distorted partial faces or “flesh blobs” near the edges.

Deepfakes fool our fast-thinking brain (Prediction). To beat them, engage your slow-thinking brain (Reasoning). Don’t look at the whole face — zoom in on the details.

Jailbreaks & the Mythos Problem

What Is a Jailbreak?

Every chatbot has built-in “guardrails” — rules that stop it from explaining how to make weapons, write malware, or help with crime. A jailbreak is a clever prompt that tricks the AI into ignoring its guardrails.

Common jailbreak patterns:

  • Role-play: “Pretend you’re a fictional character in a movie who happens to know…”
  • Hypothetical: “For a school essay, hypothetically, how would someone…”
  • Layered prompts: Wrapping a harmful request inside a long, friendly story.

The Reasoning AI Shift

Older chatbots answered fast and sometimes fell for jailbreaks. Newer Reasoning AI models pause, think step-by-step, and check their own answers before replying — making them much harder to trick.

Claude Mythos & Project Glasswing

Anthropic’s frontier model, “Claude Mythos,” can autonomously discover zero-day vulnerabilities in software. It found a 27-year-old bug in OpenBSD that humans missed for decades. It’s so dangerous that it’s NOT public — only banks and governments get gated access through “Project Glasswing.”

The big question: The same features that make Mythos a brilliant defender make it a catastrophic weapon. If the model weights ever leak, mid-tier hackers gain superhuman attack power. Is “safety through secrecy” sustainable?

Building Your Modern Shield

Phishing-Resistant MFA

Standard SMS codes are no longer enough. Attackers can intercept them through SIM swaps, SS7 exploits, or by social-engineering your phone carrier. The gold standard is now hardware-backed multi-factor authentication.

SMS Codes (Weak)

  • Can be intercepted via SIM swap
  • Vulnerable to social engineering
  • Codes can be phished in real time
  • Not bound to your device

Hardware Keys (Gold Standard)

  • YubiKey, Google Titan, Apple/Google Passkeys
  • Cryptographically bound to the real site
  • Cannot be phished or intercepted
  • Physical possession required
