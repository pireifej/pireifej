The Big Picture

The Layers of AI

In Session 3, we learned that "AI" isn't just one thing — it's a set of layers, each building on the one before it. Here's the stack:

AI
Machine Learning
Deep Learning
Generative AI

And running alongside all of them:

Responsible AI
  • AI (Artificial Intelligence): The big goal — making machines that can perform tasks that normally require human intelligence.
  • Machine Learning: A method for achieving AI. Instead of programming rules by hand, the computer learns from data. A human tells it what to look for (e.g., "look for wheels to identify a car").
  • Deep Learning: A more advanced kind of Machine Learning that uses neural networks (signals, weights, and nodes). The computer figures out on its own what features matter — no human needed to point them out.
  • Generative AI: AI that doesn't just analyze data — it creates new data. Text, images, code, music, video — all generated from patterns it learned.
  • Responsible AI: The safety manual — fairness, transparency, accountability, and privacy. It's the brakes on the car, making sure AI doesn't cause harm.

Think of it this way: AI is the goal. Machine Learning is the method. Deep Learning is the advanced method. Generative AI is the creative output. Responsible AI is the safety manual.

Discriminative vs Generative

Two Types of AI Thinking

One of the most important distinctions in AI is the difference between discriminative and generative models:

  • Discriminative AI: Looks at data and classifies it. "That's a cat." "That email is spam." "This X-ray shows a fracture." It sorts things into categories.
  • Generative AI: Creates new data that didn't exist before. "Here's a photo of a cat wearing a top hat." "Here's a 500-word essay about climate change." It produces, not just labels.

Easy way to remember: Discriminative = "What is this?"    Generative = "Make me one."

Tokens & Hallucinations

How AI Sees Words

AI doesn't read words like we do. It breaks text into tokens — chunks of characters, each assigned a number. For example, GPT-4 splits "unhappiness" into three tokens: "un" (ID: 359), "h" (ID: 71), and "appiness" (ID: 67,391).

Token IDs are assigned by frequency — common chunks get low numbers, rare chunks get high numbers. And notice: AI doesn't split words the way humans would! "un / h / appiness" is not how we'd break it up.

AI calculates numbers, not meaning. It predicts the most likely next token — and sometimes that prediction is wrong. That's a hallucination.

Putting It All Together

The Big Question

Session 3 was all about understanding that AI doesn't think — it calculates. It predicts text by finding the most probable next token. It generates images by removing noise. And when it gets something wrong, it's not lying — it's just doing math on patterns.

Quiz: Which statement best describes how an LLM (like ChatGPT) generates a response?

B It searches the internet for the best answer and copies it
C It predicts the most likely next word over and over, based on patterns learned from training data

Remember This

As John Searle (paraphrased) put it: "AI doesn't think. It calculates. And there's a world of difference between the two."

And Edsger W. Dijkstra: "The question of whether a computer can think is no more interesting than the question of whether a submarine can swim."
